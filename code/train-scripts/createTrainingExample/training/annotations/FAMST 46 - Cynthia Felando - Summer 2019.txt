officially enrolled for access) course objectives: the goals for this course are to expand what you know about film as an art, a technology, a business, and as an entertainment. 46 is also to designed to expand the ways in which you understand, comprehend, and appreciate film. your "visual literacy" and analytical skills will be developed so that you see and hear much more in films than you did before. there also is an emphasis on developing your film vocabulary to enable you to critically analyze and communicate your thoughts and ideas about film in writing and discussion. please note: this is a writing intensive course and includes several in-class writing exercises. be sure to bring paper to class for the exercises. there are only a few weeks to cover a great deal of material, so it is essential to complete the assigned readings before your discussion section. the material for the course is cumulative and builds upon what you have mastered from one week to the next. do not fall behind. note: lectures cover only some of the important concepts in the reading assignments. to assist you in the course, several study guides are available on the course website. course expectations: you should come to class on time and have your phone turned off during the entire class period. do not browse the internet during lecture or screenings. these are class requirements. if they are not observed, you may be required to leave class. also: if you use a laptop to take notes during screenings, sit in the last row or two and position yourself so that your screens light does not disturb your fellow students. screenings: screenings will be announced in class and a list of films screened will be posted on the course website. the films screened in class illustrate the key course concepts and introduce you to a variety of types of filmmaking. they are drawn from a variety of historical periods, from the early years of cinema to the present, and some of the films are from traditions that may be unfamiliar to you. you are encouraged to see every film more than once and you will be specifically asked about them on exams and in discussion on them (which is also true for the clips screened in class). be sure to take notes on the films and clips screened in class, by looking for examples that illustrate the concepts discussed in the readings and lectures. your notes will be helpful in discussion section and in preparing for exams and assignments. the films screened in this class are sometimes difficult and challenging, so taking careful notes is essential to good preparation--to get started, consult the tip sheet for notetaking on the course website. see the films when they are screened in class, even if you have already seen them. please, no talking, screens, or other distractions during screenings. grades and assignments section participation and attendance: required (10% of course grade) in-class writing exercises: required (15% of course grade) first
